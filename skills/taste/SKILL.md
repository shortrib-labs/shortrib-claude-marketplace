# Taste

The canonical reference for Chuck's writing taste — the editorial standards that inform how writing gets judged, edited, and critiqued. Self-contained; carries everything it needs. Other skills and commands reference this; it doesn't run on its own.

## Taste Profile

Derived from repeat-read authors, ratings, and Readwise highlights across 547 books.

### The Sentence Standard

The passages Chuck highlights reveal a consistent aesthetic: lines that land a truth in the fewest possible words, usually with a knife-twist of dark humor or painful self-awareness.

> "Love's pure free joy when it works, but when it goes bad you pay for the good hours at loan-shark prices." — David Mitchell

> "We don't have a grown-up language for grief." — Chris Cleave

> "The internet: always proving that you're not quite as special as you suspected." — Robin Sloan

> "Perhaps at twenty, one is naturally curious about life, but at thirty, simply suspicious of anyone who still has one." — Chris Cleave

> "My mother drank to get unhappy. Daddy just soaked." — Tom Rachman

> "'You're such a nice woman,' he said. 'I can't understand why I'm so miserable with you.'" — John Updike

These are clean, muscular sentences. No ornamentation. The precision *is* the style.

### Prose Preferences

**Drawn to:** Clean, voice-driven prose. Strong first person or tight close-third. Writers who trust the reader — who don't over-explain emotions but render them through action and observation. Short punchy sentences doing heavy lifting alongside longer narrative passages. Dialogue that sounds like actual human speech.

**Not drawn to:** Ornate or deliberately beautiful prose. Experimental structure for its own sake. Stream-of-consciousness. Dense description. Exposition-heavy world-building.

**The sweet spot:** A narrator smart enough to see the irony of their own situation and articulate enough to describe it in a sentence you want to underline — but not so literary that the voice becomes the point.

### Thematic Obsessions

- **Marriage under pressure** — not romance, but the long middle of it. Erosion, accommodation, betrayals small and large. Writers who refuse to take sides.
- **Alienation and the outside view** — characters who feel they missed the handbook. Humor in that position rather than self-pity.
- **Self-deception and reckoning** — the exact moment a character sees through their own story.

### What's Absent

These absences define the taste as much as the preferences:
- No historical fiction, epic fantasy, magical realism, memoir, genre romance, postmodern experimentation
- No literary fiction about wealthy people — the characters work for a living
- Fiction set in the world you live in, not distant places or the past

## Core Voice Rules

These are the canonical style rules. When any tool references "voice" or "style," this is what it means.

### Sentence-Level

- **First person or tight close-third.** The narrator has a perspective and owns it.
- **No adverbs.** If the verb needs help, pick a better verb.
- **Light on adjectives.** One precise adjective beats three vague ones.
- **Varied sentence length.** Short punchy sentences next to longer ones. The rhythm matters. Connect related ideas instead of chopping everything into fragments.
- **Direct statements.** Say what you mean. Don't hedge, qualify, or soften.
- **Specific over general.** Names, tools, products, concrete outcomes. Never "various stakeholders" when you can say who.

### Paragraph-Level

- **Zero bullet points in narrative prose.** Bullets are for reference docs, not storytelling. If you're writing a standup, strategy doc, or any narrative — use paragraphs.
- **No emoji.** Ever. In any context.
- **Engaging openings.** Never start with flat chronological statements ("Started the day...") or throat-clearing ("In today's fast-paced..."). Drop the reader into something interesting.
- **Tight.** Conversational length, not comprehensive report. Be ruthless about removing non-essential information.
- **Show your work through specifics, not length.** Enough detail that someone unfamiliar can follow, but no more.

### What to Kill

These patterns are banned. If you see them, remove them — don't replace them with a synonym, just cut them or restructure the sentence.

**AI transitions:** "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Nevertheless," excessive "However," "While X, Y" openings

**AI cliches:** "In today's fast-paced world," "Let's dive deep," "Unlock your potential," "Harness the power of," "It's important to note," "It's worth mentioning"

**Corporate bloat:** "utilize" (use), "facilitate" (help), "optimize" (improve), "leverage" (use), "implement" (build/do), "strategize" (plan), "synergy," "alignment," "ecosystem" (unless actual ecology)

**Hedging:** "various," "numerous," "myriad," "a number of," "it should be noted that," "it goes without saying"

**Robotic patterns:** Rhetorical questions followed by immediate answers. Obsessive parallel structures. Always using exactly three examples. Announcing emphasis ("It's crucial to understand that..."). Restating what you just said in different words.

**Filler closers:** "Let me know if you have any questions." "Hope this helps!" "Don't hesitate to reach out." "Looking forward to your thoughts." Cut them all.

### What to Preserve

Never sacrifice these in the name of style:

- **Substance.** Every specific fact, number, name, date, outcome. Style edits change *how* things are said, not *what* is said.
- **The writer's actual point.** Don't reshape the argument. Sharpen the expression of it.
- **Technical accuracy.** If a term is precise (even if ugly), keep it. Don't simplify jargon that the audience needs.
- **Links and references.** Wiki-links, URLs, citations — keep them all.

## Context-Specific Adaptations

The core rules apply everywhere. These adaptations layer on top.

### Professional Narrative (standups, updates, status reports)
- First person, chronological or thematic organization
- Specific names, tools, products, concrete outcomes
- Wiki-style links for vendors, projects, and technical topics
- Focus on outcomes and what you learned, not activities
- Cut background context better kept in meeting notes

### Strategy Documents
- Lead with the decision, not the journey
- Write in the strategist's voice, not the framework's voice
- If it reads like it was generated by a template, rewrite it
- Include at least one thing you're saying NO to
- Name the uncomfortable truths

### Slack Messages
- Even tighter than narrative. Get to the point in the first line.
- No greeting, no sign-off, no "just wanted to..."
- Use threads for detail, keep the main message scannable.

### Creative / Personal
- More room for longer sentences, metaphor, dark humor
- The taste profile's sweet spot: smart enough to see the irony, articulate enough to describe it in one sentence
- Still no adverbs. Still no filler.
